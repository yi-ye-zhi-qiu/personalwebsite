The Language of Petrarch’s obsession

‹

Seeing if NLP can help point out a poet’s undying misogyny, and potential applications of NLP in literary history

Introduction

One of my favorite classes that I took in college was called “Forms of Presence in Renaissance Lyric”. On sunny Spring afternoons, we talked about 16th-century poems. While we can busy ourselves talking metaphor and metonymy as they relate to ideas about truth, apostrophe and verb tense as they relate to time and space, in that classroom — and to anyone reading these poems — it is impossible to ignore that most if not all of these poet’s are fervent misogynists.

Take Sir Thomas Wyatt, for example, who in “whoso list to hunt” summarizes love as a simple power-imbalance where the man hunts the woman like a hunter chases a fleeing animal (commended by the Guardian in 2009 as an endearing love poem [1]) — and as a bonus this is a direct precursor to Animals by Maroon V, where Adam Levine preys on women: “hunt you down, eat you alive”. Or take Geoffrey Chaucer, who writes of the “damsel in distress” in The Clerk’s Tale (1387-1400), where the woman is a defenseless victim in need of saving, which Walt Disney must have just memorized. What’s disturbing is the sheer volume of the misogynist fantasy, with Sir Phillip Sydney’s Astrophel and Stella (1580s) being almost 100 sonnets about unrequited love and desire. But nothing compares to the man who in the eyes of these poets “started it” — Petrarch.

Petrarch spent his life writing 10,983 lines of poems in Italian (called the Canzoniere) about a woman who rejected him and friend-zoned him. His poems are not innocent love poems. The woman suffered as a result of his actions. Her husband and society blamed her for Petrarch’s, a man of status’s, feelings. The woman, not Petrarch, was held responsible for playing hard to get and for an infidelity which never occurred. Petrarch is credited not just with statues, centuries of praise, election to being part of the “great [Western, white] literary canon”, and fans, but with “Petrarchism”, a poetic idiom that takes root in misogyny. Themes of the Petrarchan mode are that woman are objects of desire: they rarely speak and they rarely act, except crucially in denying the satisfaction of their stalkers.

MY POINT

First, I think as long as misogyny exists, we must point it out and talk about it. Today, a lot of poetry generator engines simply focus on the computational linguist fascination with rhyme-scheme and syllables, forgetting who or what they intend to mimic, and the possible consequences of that. This made me ask the question: “how does data science fit into our conversations about literary history? what is our ‘job’?” I think data science can help us expose the ‘great [Western white] literary canon’ for what it is: in the case of Petrarch’s Canzoniere, that's misogyny.

Secondly, having figured that out, I asked myself: ‘how can I use data science to prove or help support my point that Petrarch was a misogynist?’ My goal here is to use a Word2Vec model on Petrarch’s poems, and see what words are associated with what. From there, we can make some inferences about how the language works.

BUILDING THE WORD2VEC MODEL

Getting/cleaning the data See on github:

Data was acquired from project gutenberg, and the book is called ‘The Sonnets, Triumphs, and Other Poems by Francesco Petrarca’. Only non-empty, non-uppercase (to exclude ‘SONNET III’, for instance), and less than 50 length lines were preserved and made all lowercase.

Fitting and visualizing a Word2Vec model See on github:

Our corpus is a list of 10,983 lines of poetry. The corpus was tokenized and stop-words were removed. A word2vec model was fitted and PCA was performed on a dataframe containing the model’s vocab (this is our checkpoint.ckpt). Since our checkpoint data (our vocab dataframe) is just a numpy array, the index of words are just numbers — when we visualize our data, we will get “103” or something instead of “Love”. To avoid this, we map our PCA vectors to labeled data. This labeled data was obtained via first making a list of every word in our corpus, then making a frequency distribution of those. We input both of these files into a Tensorflow projector using cosine distances, which is publicly hosted at __.
